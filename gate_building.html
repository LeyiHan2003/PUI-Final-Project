GATE BUILDINGS


The gate of the Quadrangle symbolizes the ranking of the house and the status of the owner. The shape, form and depth of the gate has strict regulations in ancient China.

The gate buildings are mostly located in the southeast corner and open to the south. This is because in the Eight Diagrams, the southeast is in the "Sung" position, which is the most auspicious.

The gate is the "face" of the household.

Western-style Gate
Guang Liang Gate is the gate with the highest level, found in Beijing quadrangles where highly ranked government officials in ancient China lived in. Guang Liang gate has the largest base and widest space in front of the wooden door. Its door frame is positioned between two middle columns, dividing the gate room in two halves.
Jin Zhu gate is ranked below Guang Liang Gate and above Ru Yi Gate; it appears at where rich families live. Its doorframe is close to the outside than Guang Liang gate, positioned between two hypostyle columns, pushed forwards for about 1.3 m, shrinking the space before the door. Suzhou-styled paintings often appear on the wooden doorframe of Jin Zhu gates, showing the residents' aesthetic tastes.
Most of the middle sized quadrangles have RuYi gates built; the most common gate building in Hutongs is also RuYi gates. Its door is set at the outer columns, and the wooden door is set inside the brick wall. Above the wooden door are beautiful and complex brick carvings, which are particularly detailed on the door heads and the qiang yan. On the wooden clasps there are also wood carvings. Most RuYi gates are built in the Qing Dynasty and some are built in the People’s Republic period. Its name “Ruyi” comes from the commonly seen Ruyi patterns on the brick carvings and the Chinese characters “如意”, which often appear on the clasps, meaning “good fortunes as one wishes”.
Southerner, or Man Zi gates are ranked below JinZhu gates, so its doorframe is pushed further outside, set between two outer columns. The rest of its structure and appearance are similar to Jinzhu gates and Guang Liang gates, except that there is no sparrow brace and have fewer decorations. The owner living in quadrangles with such doors usually do not hold positions in the government, but are rich merchant from Southern China.
In Peking Hutongs, common civilians live in small-scaled Quadrangles or Quadrangles with only three sides, which commonly have Embedded gates gates. It is small in size, the door is embedded inside the wall, and has a simple appearance. The walls of some embedded gates are built with broken bricks and are of low quality, so Pekingese call these gate buildings “walnut pastry”.
From later Qing dynasty until People’s Republic period, traditional northern chinese architecture was greatly affected by western culture, and many western – styled gate buildings appeared in Hutongs. These western-styles gates show both western and Chinese architectural features: most gates keep traditional wooden doors, cymbal shaped knockers, brick-carvings and door pillow stones, but there are western styled decorations on the walls and the door head. The earliest western-styled gates are built in YuanMing Yuan (the Winter palace), therefore Pekingese also call these gates YuanMing Yuan gates.
